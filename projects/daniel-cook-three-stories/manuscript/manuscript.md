# Daniel Cook: Three Stories from the Revolution

## Working Manuscript

Only a little was remembered
of Daniel Cook.

That little was heard
from father to child,
and carried on.

He served through the Revolutionary War
with England.

The war broke his constitution,
and his health was always poor.

He was in many battles
and skirmishes.

But still,
he was saved.

One night,
Daniel was sent out
with one other soldier
as a scouting party,
to see what they could find.

The night was dark.

As they went down a hill,
a stone wall stood
on both sides of the road.

Then, on the other side,
about thirty men rose up
from behind the wall.

Because they were higher
than Daniel was,
he could see them
against the sky.

"Who comes there?"
one of them called.

"Friend,"
Daniel answered.

"Friend to who?"
the voice asked again.

"Friend to the States,"
Daniel replied.

Then the enemy shouted,
"Surrender!"

At that moment,
Daniel fired his musket
at the biggest one
and ran for the opposite wall.

The moment he was on top of it,
the bullets came
like hailstones.

One went through
the top of his hat.

One went through his ear.

One grazed his side.

One went into
the heel of his shoe.

Down the hill
Daniel and the other soldier ran,
until they met
a party of their own men.

Thinking he had met
another enemy,
Daniel cried,
"I give up! I give up!"

Soon,
to his joy,
he found they were friends.

Daniel wished to go back
and pursue the enemy.

But the officer thought
it was not prudent
in the night.

So they returned to camp.

There,
Daniel tried to find
a hole in his side.

His hand was covered
with blood.

But he found
no bullet hole.

In the morning,
the men returned
to the spot.

They found blood
on the ground.

They supposed
Daniel had likely
hit his mark.

At another time,
hunger drove Daniel's company
almost to desperation.

Their horse beef was gone.

No dogs,
or any kind of meat,
could be found.

Some of the men
even roasted their shoes
and ate them.

At last,
the captain told them
to go out
and take some Tory's chickens,
or anything they could find.

They knew of a Tory nearby
who had plenty,
but would sell none.

He had a large bulldog
that guarded the house,
so there was no coming near
in the night.

At length,
a plan was made.

Daniel was selected
as guard.

He went near the house.

Out came the dog
in a great fury.

Daniel saw
that he must defend himself.

He drew his musket
and fired.

The shot brought
the old Tory to the door
in a great rage,
wanting to know
what Daniel had shot for.

Daniel said the dog
had put him in danger
of being torn to pieces
every time he was on duty.

He kept the old man there
in the cold
for some time.

Then Daniel left for camp.

There he found
the boys had got some honey,
taken from the old Tory's bee house
while the Tory was scolding
about the dog.

The next morning,
the old Tory came to camp
in pursuit of his honey.

The captain told him
he did not think
his boys had it.

"But,"
said the captain,
"we will search."

So round the camp they went,
the old man peeking
and looking very closely.

At last they came
to the mess
where the honey was hidden
in a chest.

The captain opened the chest,
put his hand suddenly
into a small cask,
and took it out again
covered with honey.

Then he slammed down the lid,
took out his handkerchief,
wiped his hand,
and said,
"Boys, what in thunder
do you keep soap here for?"

Daniel answered,
"We have to keep it there
or the soldiers
will steal it from us."

One of the mess
had his tongue swollen
from the sting of a bee,
so that his mouth
was wide open.

The old Tory wondered at this
and asked
what ailed that man.

The captain told him
he had the toothache.

This passed off,
and the old man
went away.

In this manner,
the soldiers had to live,
sometimes almost starving,
sometimes watching Tories,
and sometimes living
on horse beef,
which was their best living
until General Lafayette
came from France.

After that,
they fared well.

Just before Lafayette came,
the men were almost starved.

Daniel heard news from home
that there was pork there.

He asked the captain
to let him go home
and get some.

The captain told him
he could not spare him.

But Daniel resolved to go.

So that night,
he started.

He was gone
eight or ten days.

Then he came back
with a load of pork
on his back.

He had brought food.

But he had also left
without permission.

Daniel was taken
for deserting.

He was court-martialed
and found guilty.

Then he was asked
if he had anything to say,
why the sentence of death
should not be passed upon him.

Daniel said
he had suffered enough
to die a thousand deaths.

He had toughed it through.

If they wished to kill him
for what he had done,
they might try it.

But he would fight
his way through
and make his escape,
or lose his life.

So the officers consulted
for some time.

Finally,
Daniel was liberated.

Then he divided
his pork all around.

And they had
a good feast.
