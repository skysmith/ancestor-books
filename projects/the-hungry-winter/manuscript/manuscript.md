# Captain Daniel and the Hungry Winter

## Status

Active manuscript draft.

## Story Draft

Winter was hard on the soldiers.

The wind cut through their coats.

The ground was cold.

The days were long.

And the food was almost gone.

Daniel Cook and the men in his company had marched and watched and waited through hunger that seemed to follow them everywhere.

At first they had horse beef.

When that was gone, they searched for anything else they could eat.

But no dogs or any kind of meat could be found.

Still the hunger stayed.

It pressed and ached and would not let go.

At last the men grew so desperate they roasted their shoes and ate them.

Even that was not enough.

Faces grew hollow.

Steps grew slow.

Tempers grew short.

The soldiers were hungry in the morning.

Hungry at noon.

Hungry again by night.

At last their captain spoke.

"Go out and take some Tory's chickens," he said, "or anything you can find."

The men knew of a Tory nearby.

He had food.

But he would sell none.

So the soldiers made their plan.

They would go quietly.

They would go carefully.

They would go in the dark.

Daniel was chosen to stand guard.

The others moved ahead through the night.

The air was sharp and still.

Somewhere far off, a branch cracked.

No one spoke.

No one laughed.

Hunger had taken all the extra words.

Daniel listened.

He watched.

He waited.

The men were not looking for glory.

They were looking for supper.

War could be that way.

One day brought musket fire and bullets.

Another brought an empty stomach and a long cold dark.

Still the soldiers went on.

Still they watched.

Still they obeyed.

Because morning always came,

whether there was food enough to meet it or not.

And that night, Daniel Cook went out into the dark with the others,

hoping they might find enough to keep marching one more day.
